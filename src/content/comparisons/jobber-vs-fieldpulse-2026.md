<p><strong>Jobber and FieldPulse both handle scheduling, invoicing, and job management for field service businesses, but they target different problems. Jobber starts at $49/month and leads on marketing tools and client communication. FieldPulse starts around $99/month and wins on flat-rate pricing books, equipment tracking, and purchase orders. Your trade and crew size determine which one actually fits.</strong></p>

<h2>What You're Actually Comparing</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber is a mature, well-funded platform with a polished interface and strong client-facing tools. FieldPulse is a trade-focused competitor built for contractors who live by flat-rate pricing and need equipment tracking baked in. Both connect to QuickBooks, both have solid mobile apps, and both will get your operation off spreadsheets and into something functional.</strong></p>

<p>Jobber has been in this space for over a decade. They've built the brand recognition, the integrations, and the onboarding experience that makes it easier for a first-time software buyer to get running fast. That's not nothing. When you're already juggling eight service calls before noon, you don't have time to fight a setup wizard.</p>

<p>FieldPulse came up after Jobber and has been carving out ground with HVAC techs, plumbers, and electricians who want flat-rate pricing books built directly into the platform rather than bolted on as an afterthought. If your business runs on a service price menu, that distinction matters more than any amount of marketing-automation features.</p>

<p>For solo operators, Jobber's Core plan at $49/month is hard to beat. <p>Once you add technicians, the math shifts. Jobber's Connect plan at $129/month covers up to 5 users flat. If you've got 3 techs on the road, you're splitting $129 across a 4-person operation. FieldPulse's per-user fees at $35-40 per person start stacking fast at that headcount.</p>

<h2>Mobile App and Day-to-Day Usability</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber's mobile app is more polished and has been iterated longer. FieldPulse's app handles field tasks well and gets consistently solid reviews from technicians who use it daily. Both apps are functional enough that your techs won't be calling you from the driveway because the software crashed.</strong></p>

<p>Your techs are going to use this app every day, not you. That's worth keeping in mind when you're evaluating platforms from a desktop browser on a Tuesday afternoon. The question isn't "does this look good in a walkthrough demo." It's "can my 54-year-old HVAC tech pull up a job, log his time, and send an invoice without calling the office."</p>

<p>Jobber's app has more polish. The navigation is clean, the layout makes sense on a phone screen, and it's clearly been designed with the field user in mind, not just the office. App store ratings back this up consistently.</p>

<p>FieldPulse's app is solid but occasionally shows its smaller-team origins. The feature depth is there, especially around flat-rate pricing and equipment tracking on the job, but some users find the interface slightly less intuitive on first contact. Most teams get comfortable within a week or two.</p>

<p>Offline functionality is worth asking both vendors about before you sign. If your techs work in areas with spotty coverage, that's a real concern, not a hypothetical one. Jobber handles limited offline access. FieldPulse has similar limitations. Neither is fully offline-capable, so if you're dispatching trucks into rural areas regularly, test both on your actual routes before committing.</p>

<h2>QuickBooks and Accounting Integration</h2>

<p><strong>Both Jobber and FieldPulse connect to QuickBooks Online. Jobber's integration is more mature and better documented. FieldPulse's works but can require more manual configuration on setup. If your bookkeeper lives in QuickBooks and you need a zero-friction sync, Jobber gives you fewer headaches.</strong></p>

<p>QuickBooks integration is one of those things you don't think about until payroll week. Then it's the only thing you think about.</p>

<p>Jobber has been connected to QuickBooks Online for years and the sync is reliable. Invoices, payments, and customer records flow between the two platforms with minimal friction. Your bookkeeper can stay in QuickBooks while your team works in Jobber. If you're on the fence about whether to run them side by side or use one over the other entirely, this comparison of <a href="/comparisons/jobber-vs-quickbooks-2026">Jobber versus QuickBooks for field service businesses</a> lays that out clearly.</p>

<p>FieldPulse also connects to QuickBooks Online and the integration works. Some users report needing more time to configure the field mapping correctly on the FieldPulse side before things sync cleanly. If you have someone technical helping with setup, this isn't a dealbreaker. If you're doing it alone on a Saturday afternoon with a deadline, Jobber's setup is more forgiving.</p>

<h2>Who Should Use Jobber</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber fits home service businesses that are actively growing their customer base and want automated follow-ups, online booking, and client communication built into their platform. It's the better pick when you need software that handles the marketing and communication side, not just dispatch and invoicing.</strong></p>

<p>If you're running a 4-truck residential crew and you're serious about generating Google reviews, re-engaging old customers with automated campaigns, and letting homeowners book appointments without calling your office line, Jobber is built for that workflow. The Grow plan is expensive at $249/month, but it consolidates tools you'd otherwise be paying for separately.</p>

<p>Residential cleaning, landscaping, and general home services tend to get the most mileage out of Jobber's client-facing features. The client hub where homeowners can view estimates, approve quotes, and pay invoices online is genuinely well-designed. If you're running 15-20 residential jobs a week, reducing inbound calls with self-service options has measurable value on the admin side.</p>

<p>Jobber also has a larger support community, more third-party integrations, and more video tutorials than FieldPulse. If you run into a problem at 7pm before a big job day, there's a decent chance someone has already posted a solution. The full <a href="/reviews/jobber-review-2026">Jobber review for 2026</a> covers where the platform delivers and where it falls short in more depth.</p>

<h2>Who Should Use FieldPulse</h2>

<p><strong>FieldPulse fits HVAC, plumbing, and electrical contractors who price jobs from a flat-rate catalog, track equipment at customer properties, and need purchase orders built into their workflow. It's built for tradespeople running service calls, not residential landscapers optimizing for Google reviews.</strong></p>

<p>If your techs are standing in front of homeowners quoting from a flat-rate book, FieldPulse makes that process faster and less error-prone. You build the pricing catalog once. Your tech pulls up the task, the customer sees the price, and you move on. No math, no phone calls back to the office, no awkward pauses.</p>

<p>The equipment tracking feature is a legitimate differentiator for HVAC shops in particular. Being able to log what equipment is installed at each property, attach service history to specific units, and pull that record up when the truck rolls out for a return visit means your tech actually knows what they're walking into. That kind of context changes a customer relationship from a transaction to something closer to a long-term account.</p>

<p>Purchase orders matter more than people think on the accounting side. Tracking which parts were ordered, from which supplier, and what job they went to saves real money at tax time and prevents the supplier-billing disagreements that tend to show up when things get busy. FieldPulse has this built in. Jobber doesn't.</p>

<h2>Final Verdict</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber wins for residential service businesses focused on growth, client communication, and marketing automation. FieldPulse wins for trade contractors who need flat-rate pricing, equipment tracking, and purchase orders built into the platform. Neither is objectively better. They're solving different problems for different operations.</strong></p>

<p>Here's how to think through the decision:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Solo operator or very small crew on a budget: Jobber Core at $49/month is the cheaper starting point. FieldPulse costs more to get started.</li>
  <li>HVAC, plumbing, or electrical running flat-rate pricing: FieldPulse has the tools Jobber doesn't. The pricing books and equipment tracking alone make it worth the higher entry price for the right operation.</li>
  <li>Residential service business chasing online bookings and review growth: Jobber Grow is where those tools live. FieldPulse doesn't compete on that side.</li>
  <li>Small teams in the 3-5 user range: Run the actual math. Jobber's Connect plan at $129/month for up to 5 users can come out cheaper than FieldPulse's per-user model depending on your headcount.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Q: What's the monthly price difference between Jobber and FieldPulse?</h3>
<p>Jobber starts at $49/month while FieldPulse begins around $99/month, making Jobber roughly $50 cheaper per month. However, total cost depends on which add-ons each business needs, and FieldPulse's price often delivers better ROI for contractors who rely on flat-rate pricing daily.</p>
<h3>Q: Does FieldPulse really have better flat-rate pricing than Jobber?</h3>
<p>FieldPulse builds flat-rate pricing books directly into its core platform, while Jobber treats flat-rate menus as a secondary feature. For HVAC, plumbing, and electrical contractors, FieldPulse's native approach means faster quoting and fewer manual workarounds in the field.</p>
<h3>Q: Can you track equipment and tools in both platforms?</h3>
<p>Only FieldPulse includes built-in equipment tracking and asset management for tools, vehicles, and job-site assets. Jobber lacks this feature entirely, forcing contractors to use separate spreadsheets or third-party tools for equipment inventory.</p>
<h3>Q: Why is FieldPulse nearly twice the price if both handle scheduling and invoicing?</h3>
<p>FieldPulse's higher cost reflects trade-specific features—flat-rate pricing books, equipment tracking, and purchase orders—that save hours for contractors managing multiple crews. Jobber's lower price and stronger automated marketing features make it better for service businesses focused on customer acquisition and retention.</p>
