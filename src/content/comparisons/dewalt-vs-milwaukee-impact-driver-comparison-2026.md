<p><strong>The Milwaukee M18 FUEL 2853-20 ($129 bare tool) beats the DeWalt DCF887 ($99 bare tool) in raw torque and mode control. But if you're already running a 20V DeWalt fleet, the DCF887 gives you 90% of the performance at a lower per-tool cost. For fresh builds or crews doing heavy commercial work, Milwaukee is the better investment.</strong></p>

<h2>Overview of Both Drivers</h2>

<p><strong>The DeWalt DCF887 is a 20V MAX XR brushless impact driver with 1,825 in-lbs of torque and three speed settings. The Milwaukee M18 FUEL 2853-20 delivers 2,000 in-lbs with four operating modes including Precision Tork. Both weigh under 3 lbs. The performance gap is narrower than the price difference suggests.</strong></p>

<p>These two brands have been fighting over tool belt space for decades. Walk into any supply house in 2026 and you'll see both logos on every jobsite. The question isn't which brand is better in some abstract sense. It's which tool is right for the kind of work you're doing and the fleet you're already running.</p>

<p>The DeWalt DCF887 has been in the lineup for years and it's earned its spot. It runs on DeWalt's 20V MAX XR platform, which means if you've got a crew with drills, circular saws, and reciprocating saws all on 20V, adding impact drivers to that ecosystem keeps your charger situation sane. Three-speed settings cover most job types from finish work up to heavy bolting. The three-LED halo work light illuminates the fastening area from multiple angles, which matters more than people admit when you're wedged under an HVAC unit in a crawlspace at an awkward angle.</p>

<p>Milwaukee's 2853-20 is the current flagship 1/4" impact driver in the M18 FUEL lineup. M18 is Milwaukee's professional-tier battery ecosystem and these batteries aren't cheap, but the tools on this platform are legitimately good. The standout feature is the four-mode system: Precision Tork mode gives you a low-speed, low-torque setting specifically designed for driving smaller screws without stripping. Electricians and HVAC techs doing control panel work will immediately appreciate it. If you want more context on Milwaukee's M18 performance history, the <a href="/gear/milwaukee-m18-impact-driver-review">Milwaukee M18 impact driver review</a> on this site covers earlier generations and how the line has evolved.</p>

<p>One thing worth noting before we get into the head-to-head: DeWalt also released the DCF850 ATOMIC, a more compact 20V impact driver that trades some torque for a smaller footprint. If you do a lot of work in genuinely tight spaces, electrical rough-in behind panel boards or plumbing valve work in confined areas, it's worth looking at alongside the DCF887. This comparison focuses on the DCF887 since it's the direct performance-tier competitor to the 2853-20.</p>

<h2>Feature-by-Feature Comparison</h2>

<p><strong>Milwaukee leads on torque (2,000 vs 1,825 in-lbs), max speed (3,600 vs 3,250 IPM), and mode count (4 vs 3). DeWalt counters with a three-LED halo work light that outperforms Milwaukee's single LED in dark spaces. The biggest gap is Milwaukee's Precision Tork mode, which DeWalt has no equivalent for at this price point.</strong></p>

<p>The torque and IPM gap is real but smaller than it looks on paper. Under normal residential service conditions, driving sheet metal screws, hex bolts for equipment anchoring, standard wood screws, you're not going to feel 175 in-lbs of difference in your wrist. The gap shows up in high-volume production work: framing a metal building, anchoring rooftop HVAC equipment, or running lots of lag screws for solar mounting hardware. In those situations, the Milwaukee will outpace the DeWalt noticeably by end of day.</p>

<p>The LED situation is worth a mention and it's the one area where DeWalt genuinely wins. DeWalt's three-LED halo ring lights the work area from multiple angles and reduces shadow when you're at an awkward angle on a fastener. Milwaukee's single LED is bright but directional. Not a dealbreaker, but if you spend a lot of time in dark mechanical rooms, under sinks, or working inside panel enclosures, that halo ring is a practical advantage you'll notice after six months, not on day one.</p>

<p>The battery situation is where the real cost lives. If you're starting from zero, the Milwaukee investment is higher up front. If you're building out a complete Milwaukee fleet, buying into M18 now makes every future tool purchase cheaper because you're not buying batteries separately for each tool category.</p>

<p>Same logic applies to DeWalt's 20V MAX system. If your crew already runs three 20V drills, a couple of circular saws, and a reciprocating saw, adding DCF887 impact drivers to that fleet is a $99 decision per truck, not a $229 decision. <h2>Who Should Pick Which</h2>

<p><strong>Pick DeWalt if you're already on 20V MAX, running a price-sensitive multi-truck operation, or doing general residential service where torque demands are moderate. Pick Milwaukee if you're building a new fleet from scratch, doing precision electrical or HVAC control work where Precision Tork mode matters, or running production-volume commercial jobs where the extra torque shows up at end of day.</strong></p>

<h3>Buy the DeWalt DCF887 If...</h3>

<ul>
  <li>You already own 20V MAX XR batteries. The bare tool at $99-$109 makes it an easy add to an existing fleet with no new ecosystem cost.</li>
  <li>You're equipping a 3-5 truck crew and the $30-$50 per tool price gap matters at scale.</li>
  <li>Your work is mostly residential service: HVAC tune-ups, plumbing repairs, light electrical. The 1,825 in-lbs handles 90% of what these calls require.</li>
  <li>You work frequently in dark mechanical rooms, crawlspaces, or attic units. The three-LED halo ring is a genuine practical advantage over Milwaukee's single LED in these conditions.</li>
  <li>You want to test the DeWalt ecosystem before committing to a full battery platform investment.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Buy the Milwaukee M18 FUEL 2853-20 If...</h3>

<ul>
  <li>You're starting fresh with no existing battery platform. Build on M18 from the beginning and every future tool purchase gets cheaper.</li>
  <li>Your techs do precision work in control panels, switchgear, or HVAC controls where stripping a small screw creates rework and callbacks. Precision Tork mode alone is worth the price difference for this use case.</li>
  <li>You run commercial or production-volume work where the extra torque and speed show up across a full eight-hour day.</li>

<h3>Scenario: 3-Truck HVAC Crew</h3>

<p>If you're running three HVAC trucks doing residential service calls, tune-ups, and light commercial maintenance, the DeWalt DCF887 is the honest answer. Equip all three trucks, buy a couple of extra 2.0Ah batteries per truck for all-day runtime, and you're done for around $450-$600 total. Milwaukee would run $600-$800 for the same setup. The tools are slightly better, but not $200 better for this application. The bigger efficiency wins for a 3-truck crew come from getting dispatch and scheduling right, not from which impact driver is on the belt. <h3>Scenario: Solo Electrician, 6-8 Calls Per Day</h3>

<p>Buy the Milwaukee. No hesitation. A solo electrician running service work deals with panel work, outlet and switch installations, equipment mounting, and junction box work. Precision Tork mode protects delicate fasteners in panels and enclosures, and you only need one bare tool plus two batteries to run all day. The extra $30-$50 is irrelevant at the solo level compared to the functionality gain. <h3>Scenario: Plumber or General Contractor</h3>

<p>Go with whichever battery platform you're already running. The torque and mode gap between these two tools doesn't justify switching ecosystems mid-fleet. If you're starting from zero, pick Milwaukee for the slightly better specs and broader platform longevity. Either way, the impact driver is one tool in a larger kit. The decision about which battery platform to commit to matters a lot more than which specific model you buy first.</p>

<h2>Final Verdict</h2>

<p><strong>Milwaukee's 2853-20 is the better tool on paper: more torque, more control, and Precision Tork for delicate fastening. But if you're already running a DeWalt 20V fleet and doing standard residential service work, the DCF887 delivers 90% of the performance for less money. Milwaukee is the right buy for new platform adopters and precision-heavy trades.</strong></p>

<p>Here's the honest take: the impact driver is not the decision that makes or breaks your service business. Either of these tools will handle the demands of 90% of field service work without complaint. What matters is committing to a platform, buying enough batteries so your techs aren't dead in the field by 2 PM, and keeping the tools from walking off the truck.</p>

<p>If I'm building a new truck kit from scratch today with no existing battery investment, I'm buying Milwaukee M18 FUEL. The Precision Tork mode, the slightly higher torque and IPM, and the overall build quality make it the better long-term platform. If I already have six 20V batteries in the van, I'm buying the DCF887 and spending the savings on an extra battery or two per truck.</p>

<p>Neither brand is going anywhere. Both warranties are solid. Both tools will last years with normal field use and basic maintenance. Pick the platform, not the logo.</p>
