<p><strong>Jobber costs $49/month at the entry level and $349/month for larger teams, but the plan most service businesses actually need is $149/month Connect. That price covers automated follow-ups, two-way SMS, online booking, and QuickBooks sync. Whether it's worth it depends on how many of those features you're actively using every month, not just paying for.</strong></p>

<h2>Why Jobber Costs What It Does</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber's pricing is built around feature tiers and per-user fees. Core at $49/month is functional but stripped down. Most of the automation and client communication tools that make Jobber actually useful sit behind the Connect plan at $149/month. Add users on top of that base rate, and a 3-person crew can easily push past $200/month before you factor in transaction fees.</strong></p>

<p>Here's where the frustration usually starts. You see "$49/month" and think that's a fair price for scheduling software. Then you realize automated follow-up emails aren't on Core. Neither is online booking, two-way texting, or the client hub. Those are the features that actually save time. So you bump to Connect, and the bill more than triples before your second tech even logs in.</p>

<p>Per-user pricing compounds the problem. Jobber's Connect plan includes a limited number of users in the base price, but once you're staffing a 4-5 person HVAC crew with a dispatcher plus field techs, you're paying add-on fees. A realistic 5-person shop on Connect with extra seats can land at $250-$300/month without trying hard.</p>

<p>Jobber Payments adds another layer. If you process cards through the platform, they take a cut on each transaction, typically around 2.9% plus $0.30. For a plumbing business running $25,000/month in card volume, that's $750/month in processing fees on top of the subscription. It's not predatory, it's standard, but people don't always see it coming when they're evaluating the monthly cost.</p>

<p>Annual billing does take roughly 30% off the listed monthly rates, which helps. But that requires a meaningful upfront commitment to a platform you may still be learning. For a detailed breakdown of what each tier actually includes and whether the feature list holds up in practice, the <a href="/reviews/jobber-review-2026">Jobber Review 2026</a> covers it tier by tier.</p>

<h2>What to Look For If You're Re-Evaluating Your Software</h2>

<p><strong>The features that separate genuinely useful FSM software from expensive-but-underused are automated client follow-ups, a mobile app your techs will open without being asked, real two-way QuickBooks sync, and a scheduling board that reads clearly on a phone screen. Pricing model matters too. Per-user pricing punishes growth; flat-fee plans become more competitive the bigger your crew gets.</strong></p>

<p>Mobile app quality is probably the thing most people underestimate when switching platforms. Your techs are updating job status from driveways, collecting signatures at front doors, and taking photos in attics. If the app requires a strong signal or freezes mid-job, it gets abandoned. An abandoned app is worse than no app, because you end up with incomplete job records and no one knows why.</p>

<p>QuickBooks sync quality varies more than most people expect. Some platforms do a one-way push of invoices to QuickBooks. Others do a genuine two-way sync that pulls customer records back into the FSM tool as well. That difference matters a lot if your office runs everything out of QuickBooks and you don't want to re-enter customer info in two places.</p>

<p>The pricing model itself is worth thinking through carefully before you switch. If you're at 2-3 users now but realistically adding staff in the next 12 months, a per-user platform could cost you significantly more than a flat-fee alternative by the end of the year. Running that math upfront saves a painful mid-year platform migration.</p>

<p>If the question is specifically whether Jobber's price is defensible for your size of business, <a href="/reviews/is-jobber-worth-it-home-service-business-2026">this breakdown on Jobber's value for home service businesses</a> goes deeper on that by crew size and trade type.</p>

<h2>The Best Jobber Alternatives in 2026</h2>

<p><strong>The five strongest Jobber alternatives for 2026 are Housecall Pro (closest feature match, strong mobile experience), Service Fusion (unlimited users at a flat rate), Workiz (built-in calling and SMS for high-volume dispatch operations), Kickserv (genuinely free plan for solo operators), and FieldPulse (competitive pricing for multi-trade and subcontractor-heavy businesses). Each targets a different budget and crew size.</strong></p>

<h3>1. Housecall Pro</h3>

<p>Housecall Pro is the most direct Jobber competitor on this list. Same target market, similar feature set, overlapping price range. Basic starts at $79/month for a single user, Essentials runs $189/month for growing teams, and Max is $299/month and up for larger operations.</p>

<p>Where Housecall Pro edges Jobber: the consumer-facing booking experience is cleaner, the in-app customer messaging is tighter, and consumer financing through Wisetack is built directly into the platform rather than bolted on through a third party. If you're running a residential HVAC or plumbing business and your clients are homeowners, Housecall Pro's front-end experience tends to land better on their side of the transaction.</p>

<p>Where it falls short: reporting is less robust than Jobber at the same price tier, and some users find the dispatching board cluttered when you're juggling more than 8-10 jobs at once on mobile. For commercial work with more complex job requirements, it doesn't scale as smoothly as Jobber's Grow tier.</p>

<p>For a head-to-head look at both platforms in everyday use, the <a href="/comparisons/jobber-vs-housecall-pro">Jobber vs Housecall Pro breakdown</a> runs through the real differences. The short version: if Jobber Connect at $149/month is where you'd land, Housecall Pro Essentials at $189/month is the equivalent. Different interface, comparable price, real tradeoffs in both directions.</p>

<p><em>Best for:</em> Residential service businesses (HVAC, plumbing, cleaning, pest control) with 1-8 techs focused on homeowner clients.</p>

<h3>2. Service Fusion</h3>

<p>Service Fusion runs flat-fee pricing across all plans: Starter at $195/month, Plus at $295/month, Pro at $495/month. All three tiers include unlimited users. That changes the math entirely once you get past 4-5 people on a team.</p>

<p>If you're running a 6-tech electrical crew and looking at Jobber's Grow plan at $349/month plus per-user fees, Service Fusion's Starter at $195/month with no headcount limit starts looking very different. The platform handles dispatching and inventory tracking well, which trades like electrical, HVAC, and plumbing tend to care about more than some of the client-communication polish that Jobber prioritizes.</p>

<p>The honest weakness: the mobile app experience is not as smooth as Jobber or Housecall Pro. Some techs find it clunky to navigate in the field, and the UI shows its age in places. If your crew is used to Jobber's mobile interface, expect a real adjustment period and some complaints during the first few weeks.</p>

<p><em>Best for:</em> Teams of 5 or more where per-seat pricing is eating budget, particularly trades with higher inventory and dispatch complexity.</p>

<h3>3. Workiz</h3>

<p>Workiz built its reputation in the locksmith and garage door market, where inbound call volume is high and speed-to-dispatch matters. The feature that sets it apart from the rest of this list is built-in VoIP calling and SMS from inside the platform, with every call logged directly to the job record. If you're running a business where phone calls drive a significant percentage of bookings, that operational advantage is real.</p>

<p>Pricing starts with a limited free plan and scales up through team tiers that run roughly $150-$225/month depending on features and users. Entry-level pricing is generally lower than Jobber's Connect plan, though the upper tiers are competitive rather than dramatically cheaper.</p>

<p>The <a href="/comparisons/workiz-vs-jobber-2026">Workiz vs Jobber comparison</a> covers where each platform wins. The short version: Workiz wins on communication tools and lower cost of entry. Jobber wins on polished client-facing workflows and a more complete overall experience for businesses that have moved beyond heavy phone dispatch.</p>

<p><em>Best for:</em> High-call-volume service businesses including locksmith, garage door, appliance repair, and cleaning companies that book heavily by phone.</p>

<h3>4. Kickserv</h3>

<p>Kickserv is the answer if you're a solo operator or running a 2-person shop and paying $149/month for Jobber feels like leasing a truck you don't drive. The free plan handles 2 users with basic scheduling and invoicing included. Paid plans start at $19/month and top out at $99/month for the Growth tier, which includes more automation and reporting.</p>

<p>It is not as feature-rich as Jobber. The automation tools are simpler, the reporting is basic, and the mobile app is functional but not impressive. That is fine if you're doing 6-8 jobs a day solo and need to track quotes, schedule work, and send invoices without paying for a platform built for a 10-person operation. Kickserv covers that use case cleanly at a fraction of Jobber's cost.</p>

<p><em>Best for:</em> Solo operators, owner-operators, and shops under 3 people where budget is the primary driver and feature depth can be sacrificed.</p>

<h3>5. FieldPulse</h3>

<p>FieldPulse gets less attention than it deserves. Pricing starts around $99/month for small teams and scales with users, but it tends to come in cheaper than Jobber at equivalent feature levels. The mobile app is well-regarded, the quoting workflow is clean, and it handles subcontractor management and job costing better than most tools in this price range.</p>

<p>It's a particularly good fit for general contractors or multi-trade shops that need to assign work to different crews or track subcontractors alongside W-2 staff. If subcontractor handling has been a specific frustration with Jobber, FieldPulse is worth a demo before you commit to something else.</p>

<p><em>Best for:</em> General contractors, multi-trade businesses, and any operation managing subcontractors regularly alongside their own crew.</p>

<h2>How to Choose the Right One</h2>

<p><strong>Match the software to your actual crew size and job volume, not the crew size you aspire to have in two years. A solo plumber doesn't need Connect-level features. A 6-person team paying per-user fees is probably overpaying for something with a flat-fee equivalent. Run the math on what you're paying per active user per month, then compare that number against alternatives at the same feature level.</strong></p>

<p>If you're a 1-2 person operation doing under 60 jobs a month, Kickserv's free or low-cost plan covers you. There is no compelling reason to pay $149/month for automation tools you don't have the volume to use effectively. Get your processes stable first, then add software horsepower when the job count justifies it.</p>

<p>If you're running 3-5 techs in residential home services, Jobber Connect and Housecall Pro Essentials are both legitimate choices. The difference usually comes down to which interface your team will actually adopt, not which one looks better in a product demo. Run a real 14-day trial with actual customer names, real job types, and your actual crew. Fake data in a trial environment tells you almost nothing about adoption.</p>

<p>Once you're past 5 techs, Service Fusion's flat-fee model deserves a serious look. The math shifts pretty clearly at that headcount. The mobile app isn't as polished, but a slightly clunkier app that doesn't add $40/user/month in overage fees is often the better call for the business overall.</p>

<p>If budget is the main driver and you want to see everything available at the low end of the market, <a href="/guides/cheapest-field-service-software">this roundup of the cheapest field service software that actually works</a> covers options most people overlook when they're only comparing the big names.</p>

<h2>Our Recommendation</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber is priced the way it is because the Connect plan is where the real product lives, and most service businesses need Connect. If you're actively using automated follow-ups, online booking, and the client hub every week, $149/month is defensible. If you're not using those features, you're paying Connect prices for Core-level work, and that's a straightforward problem to fix.</strong></p>

<p>For most residential service businesses running 3-5 techs, Jobber Connect or Housecall Pro Essentials are both solid choices. Neither is clearly better in every scenario. Jobber has cleaner workflows and a slightly more polished client-facing experience. Housecall Pro has better consumer-side booking and built-in financing. Pick the one your team will actually use and stop overthinking the comparison.</p>

<p>For solo operators or 1-2 person shops where $149/month is a real budget concern: start on Kickserv's free or low-cost plan. It's not as capable as Jobber, but it handles the basics without requiring you to pay for features you're not using yet. You can always migrate once the revenue justifies it.</p>

<p>For teams of 6 or more where per-user pricing has become a monthly irritation: run the Service Fusion numbers against what you're currently paying. Most shops in that range find the flat-fee math works out better, even with the tradeoff in mobile polish.</p>

<p>Jobber is genuinely good software. Whether it's the right software at the right price for your specific operation is a different question, and the answer varies by trade, crew size, and how much of the feature set you're actually putting to work on a weekly basis.</p>
<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>Q: What fees does Jobber charge besides the monthly subscription?</h3>
<p>Jobber charges per-user add-on fees for team members beyond the base allocation on your plan, and if you process payments through Jobber Payments, you'll pay approximately 2.9% plus $0.30 per transaction. For a business processing $25,000 monthly in card sales, payment processing alone costs around $750/month on top of your subscription.</p>

<h3>Q: How much do extra Jobber users cost per month?</h3>
<p>Add-on user pricing varies by plan, but costs escalate quickly with field teams. A 5-person crew with a dispatcher on the Connect plan typically needs multiple extra seats, easily reaching $250–$300/month when you factor in the base subscription plus additional user fees.</p>

<h3>Q: Is the $49/month Jobber Core plan actually functional for service businesses?</h3>
<p>Core is stripped down—it lacks automated follow-ups, online booking, two-way SMS, and the client hub, which are the features that actually save time and justify the cost. Nearly all service businesses end up upgrading to Connect at $149/month to access the core functionality they need.</p>

<h3>Q: How much can you save by paying Jobber annually instead of monthly?</h3>
<p>Annual billing typically discounts the monthly rate by roughly 30%, but requires a significant upfront commitment. On the Connect plan, that translates from $149/month down to approximately $105/month, which makes sense if you're confident in the platform long-term.</p>
