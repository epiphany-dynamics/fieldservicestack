<p><strong>The best appliance repair dispatch software in 2026 depends on your shop size. Workiz is the top pick for most appliance repair businesses, starting around $65/user/month with job tracking built for equipment history and repeat customers. Jobber is the easiest entry point for solo techs at $49/month. ServiceTitan has the deepest features but costs 3-5x more and requires a real commitment.</strong></p>

<h2>What to Look For in Appliance Repair Dispatch Software</h2>

<p><strong>Appliance repair dispatch software needs equipment history, parts tracking, and fast scheduling in one place. Look for appliance-specific job fields (model, serial number, brand), solid customer communication tools, a mobile app your tech can actually use in a cramped utility room, and clean accounting integration. Dispatch optimization and customer history are what separate useful tools from frustrating ones.</strong></p>

<p>Appliance repair has quirks that make generic field service software annoying to work with. You're dealing with repeat customers across multiple machines, warranty jobs mixed with out-of-pocket calls, and parts that may or may not be in the truck when the tech pulls up. A platform that doesn't let you log the make and model of the appliance is going to create headaches by job three.</p>

<p>Here's what actually matters when you're evaluating these tools:</p>

<ul>
<li>Job scheduling with appliance-specific fields: model number, serial number, brand, reported symptom</li>
<li>Customer history so you can pull up what you serviced last time in under 10 seconds when someone calls</li>
<li>Parts tracking, or at minimum a way to log parts used on a job without writing it on a napkin</li>
<li>A mobile app that works with spotty signal (basements, rural areas, thick concrete walls)</li>
<li>Customer notifications via text and email for appointment reminders and en-route alerts</li>
<li>In-field invoicing and payment collection</li>
<li>QuickBooks or Xero integration that doesn't require manual sync babysitting</li>
</ul>

<p>For a solo appliance tech doing 8-10 calls a day, dispatch optimization matters less than raw speed. You need to create a job, schedule it, close it out, and get paid with as few taps as possible. For a 5-truck shop, you need a real dispatch board, tech locations, and the ability to reassign a call when one guy gets buried on a refrigerant system job that's running two hours over.</p>

<p>If you're evaluating platforms beyond just dispatch, the <a href="/guides/scheduling-software-for-service-technicians-2026">scheduling software guide for service technicians</a> covers what to look for across most trades and business sizes.</p>

<h2>Top Appliance Repair Dispatch Software for 2026</h2>

<p><strong>Workiz is the strongest all-around pick for appliance repair shops. Jobber is the easiest to get up and running fast. Housecall Pro sits between the two on features and price, with better customer communication tools. Service Fusion is worth a look if you're running a larger team and want flat-rate pricing. ServiceTitan is enterprise-grade and priced accordingly.</strong></p>

<h3>1. Workiz</h3>

<p>Workiz is one of the few platforms that actually feels like it was designed with appliance repair in mind. The job form lets you capture appliance make, model, and symptom. The dispatch board is clean. The customer timeline gives you the full service history at a glance when someone calls about the washing machine you worked on eight months ago.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Free plan available for up to 2 users (limited to basic features, not enough for a real operation). Paid plans start around $65/user/month, with team bundles that reduce per-user cost as you add techs. Annual billing saves roughly 20%.</p>

<p><strong>What's good:</strong> The two-way texting is genuinely useful in practice. Customers can confirm, reschedule, or ask a quick question without calling the office. Reviews are built in and trigger automatically after a job closes. The mobile app is one of the better ones in this space and holds up with spotty signal. The dispatch board gives you drag-and-drop scheduling with tech availability visible at a glance without clicking into five different screens.</p>

<p><strong>What's not:</strong> The free plan is essentially a demo. The jump to paid stings if you're a one-truck shop watching margins. QuickBooks integration works, but it occasionally needs a manual sync nudge to stay clean. And the pricing structure can get complicated once you start adding team members at different tiers.</p>

<p>If you're already comparing Workiz to similar tools, the <a href="/comparisons/workiz-vs-jobber-2026">Workiz vs. Jobber breakdown</a> goes deeper on where each one pulls ahead.</p>

<h3>2. Jobber</h3>

<p>Jobber is the default recommendation for appliance repair shops that want solid software without a learning curve that eats two weeks of your time. The interface is clean, setup takes a few hours, and the Core plan at $49/month covers one user doing everything solo. If you're a one-truck operation, this is a hard price to argue against.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Core $49/month (1 user), Connect $149/month (up to 5 users), Grow $299/month (up to 15 users). All billed monthly, with discounts available on annual plans.</p>

<p><strong>What's good:</strong> Client management is excellent. Job history, equipment notes, custom fields, client tags, it's all there and actually organized in a way that makes sense. The client hub lets customers view their job status and pay online, which cuts down on "did you get my check" calls significantly. QuickBooks Online integration is native and the cleanest on this list. The mobile app consistently gets top ratings in the field service category and the techs pick it up without needing a training session.</p>

<p><strong>What's not:</strong> The Core plan only covers one user. The second you have a tech plus an office admin, you're jumping to $149/month. Appliance-specific fields like model number and serial aren't built in by default. You can add them as custom fields, but it takes setup work that shouldn't be necessary for a trade-specific platform. Two-way texting is only on the Connect plan and above.</p>

<p>For a more complete look at whether Jobber holds up under real workloads, the <a href="/reviews/jobber-review-2026">full Jobber review</a> covers where the platform starts to show its limits at scale.</p>

<h3>3. Housecall Pro</h3>

<p>Housecall Pro is the most consumer-friendly platform on this list. Easy setup, a polished interface, and strong customer communication tools. If your biggest operational pain points are appointment no-shows and slow invoice payments, Housecall Pro addresses both better than most competitors.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Basic $79/month (1 user), Essentials $189/month (up to 5 users), MAX is custom pricing for larger teams. Monthly billing is available; annual saves around 15-20%.</p>

<p><strong>What's good:</strong> Automated customer communication is the best in class here. Appointment reminders, review requests, follow-up messages, it all runs on autopilot once you set it up. The online booking widget that goes on your website is well designed and customers actually use it. Dispatching is intuitive. Techs figure out the mobile app fast without hand-holding. Online payments work cleanly and customers don't complain about the interface.</p>

<p><strong>What's not:</strong> At $79/month for one user, it's priced higher than Jobber's $49 Core plan with less to show for it at the entry tier. The Basic plan doesn't include route optimization, which matters when you're running 8 stops across a metro area. Reporting is thin until you get to higher-tier plans. Like Jobber, there are no appliance-specific fields built in by default.</p>

<p>There's a head-to-head <a href="/comparisons/jobber-vs-housecall-pro">Jobber vs. Housecall Pro comparison</a> that maps out exactly where each platform wins, including pricing math for different team sizes.</p>

<h3>4. Service Fusion</h3>

<p>Service Fusion gets overlooked in most roundups, which is a mistake for shops running 4 or more techs. It's priced higher than Jobber or Workiz at the entry level, but the Starter plan at $199/month covers unlimited users. If you do the per-user math at 5 techs plus a dispatcher, Service Fusion can actually be the cheapest option on this list.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Starter $199/month, Plus $349/month, Pro $499/month. Unlimited users on all plans. No per-user fees added on top.</p>

<p><strong>What's good:</strong> The unlimited user structure is the headline feature. Flat-rate pricing with no per-user fees makes budgeting predictable as you grow. The dispatch board handles larger teams reasonably well. Estimates, work orders, invoicing, and customer records are all solid. QuickBooks integration works reliably without constant maintenance.</p>

<p><strong>What's not:</strong> The interface looks dated compared to Workiz or Jobber. It works, but it's clunky in places that should be smooth. The mobile app is the weakest on this list, which matters a lot if your techs are doing most of their job management from their phones. Customer-facing features like online booking and the client portal aren't as polished as what Housecall Pro or Jobber offer.</p>

<h3>5. ServiceTitan</h3>

<p>ServiceTitan is the most powerful platform here. It also has the steepest price, the most implementation overhead, and a contract that requires a careful read. For a 2-truck appliance repair operation, it's overkill. For a multi-location shop running 15+ techs with serious volume, it starts making sense.</p>

<p><strong>Pricing:</strong> Not published publicly. Typically starts in the $300-500/month range with implementation fees that can run $2,000-5,000. Annual contracts required. Custom pricing through a sales rep.</p>

<p><strong>What's good:</strong> The dispatch and scheduling engine is genuinely best-in-class for larger teams. Revenue reporting, marketing attribution, technician performance tracking, membership management. It's built for scale and the feature depth shows. If you're at 10 or more techs and growing, the reporting alone can justify the cost.</p>

<p><strong>What's not:</strong> The price, the contract, and the implementation timeline. Smaller shops consistently report that onboarding eats 3-4 weeks and requires someone internally to own it. You're not just buying software. You're buying a system that requires setup, training, and ongoing management. A 3-truck shop is paying for features they won't touch for years.</p>

<p>If you're seriously considering it, the <a href="/reviews/is-servicetitan-worth-it-2026">ServiceTitan value assessment</a> gives an honest take on who it's actually built for.</p>

<h2>How to Choose the Right Software for Your Shop</h2>

<p><strong>Match the software to your actual operation today, not where you hope to be in three years. A solo tech should start with Jobber Core. A 3-5 truck crew should trial Workiz. Shops with 5 or more techs should run the per-user math on Service Fusion before committing. ServiceTitan only makes sense once you've genuinely outgrown the simpler tools.</strong></p>

<p>Here's how to think through it based on where you are right now:</p>

<p>If you're a solo appliance tech doing 8-10 calls a day, priority is speed and simplicity. Jobber Core at $49/month gives you scheduling, invoicing, customer history, and QuickBooks sync. That covers most of what you need without overcomplicating a solo operation. The client hub is a bonus: customers can pay online and check job status without calling you.</p>

<p>If you're running a 3-truck crew, dispatch visibility becomes the real pain point. You need to see who's available, what's been assigned, and whether a job is going long. Workiz handles that better than Jobber at this scale, and the built-in two-way texting cuts down on "where's the tech" calls from customers. The cost runs higher per user, but the time saved on daily dispatch coordination makes the math work.</p>

<p>If you have 5 or more techs, get out a calculator before you commit. Service Fusion's unlimited user plans start to look considerably better once you hit 4-5 people. At $199/month for unlimited users, you're paying less per head than most per-user platforms. The trade-off is a weaker mobile app and a less polished interface. If your techs live in the mobile app all day, that's a real trade-off and not a minor one.</p>

<p>For multi-location shops or franchise models, ServiceTitan is the honest answer even when the price is hard to look at. Just go in with clear expectations about implementation time, contract length, and the internal resources you'll need to manage it properly.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>Q: How much does appliance repair dispatch software cost for a solo technician vs. a small team?</h3>
<p>Jobber starts at $49/month for solo techs, while Workiz charges about $65/user/month, keeping small teams under $200/month total. ServiceTitan and enterprise platforms cost 3-5x more, making them practical only after you grow to 5+ technicians where per-seat costs become reasonable.</p>

<h3>Q: Does appliance dispatch software work offline on job sites with poor cell signal?</h3>
<p>Yes—Jobber and Workiz both offer offline mode so techs can log jobs, capture photos, and collect signatures without signal in basements or rural areas. Data syncs automatically when connection returns, making offline capability essential for appliance repair shops serving older homes and remote locations.</p>

<h3>Q: How quickly can I go live with appliance repair dispatch software?</h3>
<p>Jobber and Workiz can dispatch your first jobs within 2-4 hours of signup, assuming you've imported customer data and configured your tech's phone. Enterprise platforms like ServiceTitan typically require 1-2 weeks of onboarding, training, and custom appliance-field setup before your first dispatch.</p>

<h3>Q: Can appliance repair dispatch software automatically track parts used on jobs?</h3>
<p>Most tools let you manually log parts consumed (filters, belts, seals) during service calls in job notes, but true truck-based automated inventory tracking is rare in appliance-specific software. Many shops use a hybrid approach: log parts in the dispatch tool and reconcile with a simple spreadsheet for reordering.</p>
