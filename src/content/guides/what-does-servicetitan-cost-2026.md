<p><strong>ServiceTitan doesn't publish its pricing publicly, but contractor reports consistently put the Starter tier around $125/month, Essentials at $300-400/month, and Professional setups at $500-700/month or more before add-ons. Annual contracts are required and onboarding fees typically run $500-$1,500. For businesses under 10 techs, most find it overbuilt and overpriced.</strong></p>

<h2>What to Look For in Field Service Management Software</h2>

<p><strong>Good FSM software handles dispatching, invoicing, customer communication, and accounting integration without requiring an office manager to babysit it. Pay close attention to pricing structure: per-user fees, annual-only contracts, and onboarding costs can easily double your first-year total. Mobile app quality matters as much as the desktop features, because your techs are the ones using it in the field.</strong></p>

<p>Before you compare dollar amounts, get clear on what you're actually buying. A lot of contractors sign a year-long contract based on a demo that looked great, then find out the field app crashes on Android or the QuickBooks sync pushes duplicate line items every week. By then you're locked in.</p>

<p>Here's what actually matters when you're evaluating options:</p>

<ul>
<li>Can your dispatcher drag and drop jobs on a map view and reassign a tech in under 30 seconds when someone calls in sick?</li>
<li>Does the mobile app work reliably in the field, including on weak cell connections or offline?</li>
<li>Can your techs collect payment on-site, including card processing and customer financing if you offer it?</li>
<li>Does the QuickBooks integration sync both ways, or does your bookkeeper still re-enter everything manually?</li>
<li>Does it send automated appointment reminders and on-the-way texts without someone manually triggering each one?</li>
<li>What are the contract terms? Month-to-month flexibility makes a real difference if you need to switch platforms mid-year.</li>
</ul>

<p>Also ask during any demo: what does onboarding actually look like and how long before you're fully live? ServiceTitan requires a formal multi-week onboarding process. Several of the lighter tools let you go live over a weekend. That gap matters when you're running jobs every day and can't have your office in transition for six weeks.</p>

<h2>Top Picks: Field Service Software Ranked for 2026</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber is the best value for small to mid-size field service businesses, with plans starting at $49/month and a mobile app that actually works. ServiceTitan leads on enterprise features but costs 5-10x more and requires annual contracts. Housecall Pro and Service Fusion fill the middle ground at $79-$349/month depending on team size and the features you actually need.</strong></p>

<h3>1. Jobber: Best for Small to Mid-Size Teams</h3>

<p>If you're running 1-10 techs and not yet doing $2M or more in annual revenue, Jobber is almost certainly the right answer. The <a href="/reviews/jobber-review-2026">full Jobber review</a> covers this in detail, but the short version is: it handles scheduling, dispatching, invoicing, client communication, and QuickBooks sync without making you feel like you need an IT department to manage it.</p>

<p>Pricing in 2026 runs across three tiers:</p>
<ul>
<li>Core: $49/month (1 user) - basic job management and invoicing</li>
<li>Connect: $149/month (up to 5 users) - automated reminders, online booking, QuickBooks sync</li>
<li>Grow: $249/month (up to 15 users) - job costing, two-way texting, advanced reporting</li>
</ul>

<p>The mobile app is solid on both iOS and Android. Techs can pull up job details, take before/after photos, collect signatures, and process payment without needing a strong signal for the basic stuff. For a 3-truck HVAC company or a solo plumber doing 8-10 calls a day, Jobber hits the sweet spot between functional and affordable.</p>

<p>Where it falls short: complex service agreements, flat-rate pricing books, and the deep reporting a 50-tech operation needs. If you're scaling past 15-20 techs, you'll start feeling the ceiling.</p>

<h3>2. Housecall Pro: Best for Growing Teams Who Want More Automation</h3>

<p>Housecall Pro sits between Jobber and ServiceTitan in both price and features. The <a href="/reviews/housecall-pro-review-2026">Housecall Pro 2026 review</a> gets into the specifics, but what separates it from Jobber is a stronger automation toolset: more sophisticated review request sequences, postcard marketing campaigns, and a better customer-facing portal that clients can actually use without calling your office.</p>

<p>Pricing in 2026:</p>
<ul>
<li>Basic: $79/month (1 user)</li>
<li>Essentials: $189/month (up to 5 users)</li>
<li>Max: $289/month (up to 8 users)</li>
<li>Max+: Custom pricing for larger teams</li>
</ul>

<p>Month-to-month plans are available, which matters if you're not ready to commit to a full year. The QuickBooks integration is reliable. Dispatching works well for teams up to around 15-20 techs before things start getting complicated.</p>

<p>Flat-rate pricing books aren't as polished as ServiceTitan's, and reporting gets limited at higher volumes. A mid-size electrical or plumbing company doing consistent six-figure months will probably outgrow it eventually, but it might take longer than you think.</p>

<h3>3. Service Fusion: Best Mid-Market Option for Mixed-Trade Operations</h3>

<p>Service Fusion is worth considering when you need more than Jobber or Housecall Pro can handle but aren't ready for the ServiceTitan price tag. It's designed for field service companies with more operational complexity, whether that's multiple trades, inventory management, or teams that have grown past what the lighter tools can manage cleanly.</p>

<p>Pricing in 2026:</p>
<ul>
<li>Starter: $149/month (unlimited users)</li>
<li>Plus: $199/month</li>
<li>Pro: $349/month</li>
</ul>

<p>The unlimited users model is a genuine advantage for larger teams. You're not getting charged extra for every tech you add to the system, which is how some platforms quietly inflate your monthly bill. Inventory management is solid and customer history tracking is better than most tools in this price range.</p>

<p>The mobile app has improved over the past couple of years but still feels secondary to the desktop experience. If your techs are handling lots of field documentation, test it with an actual Android device before you commit.</p>

<h3>4. ServiceTitan: Best for Enterprise-Level Operations</h3>

<p>ServiceTitan is the most powerful FSM platform on the market. It's also the most expensive and the most complex to implement. If you're running a $5M+ operation with 20 or more techs, a dedicated dispatcher, and someone managing the books full-time, it makes sense. If you're not there yet, you're buying more software than your business can actually absorb.</p>

<p>Pricing in 2026 (based on contractor reports, since ServiceTitan doesn't list rates on their site):</p>
<ul>
<li>Starter: ~$125/month base, plus $65-100/user/month in per-tech fees</li>
<li>Essentials: ~$300-400/month for small teams</li>
<li>Professional: ~$500-700/month for mid-size operations</li>
<li>Enterprise: Custom pricing, typically $1,000-2,500+/month for larger shops</li>
</ul>

<p>Add onboarding fees of $500-$1,500 to whatever you're quoted. Annual contract required. If you cancel early, you're still on the hook for the remainder of the term.</p>

<p>The feature depth is real. ServiceTitan's flat-rate pricing book, dispatching board, marketing suite, and reporting are genuinely excellent at scale. If you're evaluating whether the price is justified for your specific operation, the <a href="/reviews/is-servicetitan-worth-it-2026">honest breakdown of whether ServiceTitan is worth it in 2026</a> goes deeper on that question without the sales spin.</p>

<p>The catch most people don't talk about: you need to staff for this platform. Implementation takes weeks. Getting your techs trained and your pricebook built out takes months. A 5-truck shop where the owner handles dispatch and invoicing himself will spend more time managing the software than running the business, at least in the first six months.</p>

<h3>5. FieldEdge: Enterprise Alternative at a Lower Entry Point</h3>

<p>FieldEdge is a solid alternative for HVAC, plumbing, and electrical contractors who need enterprise-level tools but aren't ready for the full ServiceTitan commitment. The QuickBooks integration is one of the tightest in the industry, which matters a lot when you're processing 100+ invoices a week and your bookkeeper is already stretched thin.</p>

<p>Pricing in 2026: FieldEdge also doesn't publish rates publicly, but contractor reports put it at roughly $100-150/user/month with a minimum of around 3 users. That's meaningfully cheaper than ServiceTitan for comparable team sizes, depending on what you're quoted.</p>

<p>You give up some of ServiceTitan's marketing automation depth and reporting breadth. What you get in return is a cleaner implementation process and faster onboarding. If the ServiceTitan quote made you flinch, get a FieldEdge demo before you sign anything.</p>

<h2>How to Choose the Right Platform for Your Business</h2>

<p><strong>If you're billing under $1.5M annually, start with Jobber or Housecall Pro and put the cost difference back into the business. If you're at $3-5M with a full dispatch team and complex service agreements, ServiceTitan or FieldEdge starts to make financial sense. A longer feature list doesn't matter if your techs stop opening the app after week two.</strong></p>

<p>If you're a solo operator or running 2-3 techs, Jobber's Connect plan at $149/month covers everything you need. Spending $400/month on ServiceTitan at this stage is just burning money that should be going toward trucks, tools, or marketing.</p>

<p>At the 5-15 tech range, Housecall Pro or Service Fusion is where I'd look first. You get real dispatching capability, solid customer communication, and enough reporting to run the business without hiring someone just to manage the software. A lot of people in this range get pushed toward ServiceTitan before they're ready for it. The <a href="/comparisons/servicetitan-vs-jobber-2026">ServiceTitan vs. Jobber breakdown for 2026</a> is worth reading before anyone from either company's sales team gets to you.</p>

<p>Twenty or more techs with dedicated office staff is where ServiceTitan becomes worth seriously evaluating. The ROI from flat-rate pricing consistency, automated follow-up sequences, and dispatching efficiency is real at that scale. Go in clear on total cost though: your first-year bill will likely run 30-40% higher than the base quoted price once onboarding, training time, and bundled add-ons are included.</p>

<p>One thing worth checking regardless of which direction you're leaning: what do real contractors say about using it day-to-day, not just in the first month? The <a href="/guides/servicetitan-customer-reviews-2026">ServiceTitan customer reviews from 2026</a> pull together what actual users are reporting about the platform, good and bad.</p>

<h2>Our Recommendation</h2>

<p><strong>For most field service businesses in 2026, Jobber at $149-249/month is the right call. ServiceTitan is excellent software, but it's priced and designed for operations that have already built the infrastructure to use it. Buying enterprise software before you're operationally ready doesn't speed up growth; it creates overhead that slows you down.</strong></p>

<p>ServiceTitan earns its price tag for the right operation. A 30-truck HVAC company with a CSR team, a pricebook that needs to stay consistent across dozens of techs, and management that actually reviews the reporting dashboard every week? They'll get the ROI. A 6-truck plumbing company where the owner is still dispatching from his cell phone? Not yet.</p>

<p>If you're somewhere in the enterprise evaluation range, get quotes from both ServiceTitan and FieldEdge before committing. The price gap between those two is real, and the feature gap may not matter for your operation at its current size. If you're still in the sub-10-tech range, spend an afternoon with the Jobber or Housecall Pro free trials first. You might find you don't need what ServiceTitan is selling.</p>

<p>The software isn't the business. The business is the business. Get the tool that fits where you are now, not the one that fits where you hope to be in five years.</p>
<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>Q: What's the actual first-year cost for ServiceTitan when you include onboarding and setup?</h3>
<p>Beyond monthly subscriptions ($125–$700+ depending on tier), ServiceTitan charges $500–$1,500 for onboarding and implementation, and requires annual-only contracts with no month-to-month option—meaning your true first-year cost is the subscription multiplied by 12 months plus setup fees. For a Professional tier setup ($600/month), that's $7,200 in recurring costs plus onboarding, totaling roughly $8,200–$9,000 before add-ons like payment processing or third-party integrations.</p>

<h3>Q: Is ServiceTitan worth it for a small contracting business with fewer than 10 technicians?</h3>
<p>Most contractors with fewer than 10 techs report that ServiceTitan is overbuilt for their needs and pricing doesn't scale down appropriately—you're often paying for features and user seats you won't use. A smaller team may find better value in more affordable FSM platforms like Jobber or Housecall Pro, which offer flexible, per-user pricing and no long-term contract lock-in.</p>

<h3>Q: Does ServiceTitan's QuickBooks integration work both ways, or do you still need to enter data manually?</h3>
<p>While ServiceTitan integrates with QuickBooks, users report that the sync sometimes creates duplicate line items or requires manual intervention—your bookkeeper may still need to reconcile entries rather than relying on fully automated two-way syncing. Before committing, confirm with ServiceTitan how the integration handles invoices, payments, and expense categories in your specific accounting workflow.</p>

<h3>Q: Can you cancel ServiceTitan early if it's not working for your business, or are you locked into the full year?</h3>
<p>ServiceTitan requires annual contracts with no monthly opt-out, meaning you're committed for 12 months regardless of whether the platform meets your needs—a significant lock-in risk if implementation takes longer than expected or the Android app proves unreliable in your field operations. This contract structure is why detailed demos and onboarding clarity are critical before signing.</p>
