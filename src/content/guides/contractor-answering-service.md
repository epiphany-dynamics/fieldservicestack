**A contractor answering service pays for itself when the gross profit from the jobs it rescues exceeds its monthly cost. For most trades that means saving one or two calls a month. Price it against your average booked job value, not against your phone bill, and the decision stops being a guess.**

## What one booked job is worth to you

**Your answering service decision runs on one number: gross profit per booked job, not ticket price. Pull the last 90 days of closed invoices, subtract materials and technician labor, then divide by job count. That figure is what a rescued call is worth, and it is what every vendor quote gets measured against.**

Most operators skip this step and compare monthly fees to each other instead of to revenue. That is how a $300 service looks expensive and a $150 service looks smart, when neither number means anything without your own job economics.

Build the worksheet once. The example column below is illustrative only, meant to show the arithmetic, not to represent your trade:

| Input | Where to find it | Illustrative example |
|---|---|---|
| Average invoice total | Closed invoices, last 90 days | $480 |
| Direct materials per job | Job costing or supplier receipts | $95 |
| Technician labor per job | Loaded hourly rate times average job hours | $130 |
| Gross profit per job | Invoice minus materials minus labor | $255 |
| Close rate on answered inbound calls | Jobs booked divided by calls answered | 55% |
| Value per answered call | Gross profit times close rate | $140 |

That last row is the number that matters. In this illustration every inbound call that gets a human voice is worth about $140 in gross profit, whether or not it books.

### Recurring work changes the math

If you sell maintenance agreements, seasonal contracts, or route work, one saved call is not one job. A pool service caller who signs a weekly route is worth a season. A pest control caller who signs a quarterly plan is worth a year. Run the same worksheet on contract value instead of single-visit value and the break-even drops sharply.

## Where missed calls actually leak revenue

**Missed calls cluster in four predictable windows, and each one has a different fix. Knowing which window leaks hardest tells you whether you need 24/7 coverage, overflow only, or better daytime dispatch. Buying blanket coverage for a problem that lives entirely between 11am and 2pm is money burned.**

The four leak points:

1. **After hours and weekends.** Garage door spring snaps at 7pm. Septic backup Sunday morning. Nobody leaves a voicemail for a broken water heater, they call the next listing.
2. **Technician on the job.** One-truck and two-truck shops route calls to the person holding a wrench. The phone rings while a tech is on a roof or in a crawlspace, and it goes unanswered.
3. **Dispatch overflow.** Storm days, first cold snap, first heat wave. Your office manager is competent and still cannot hold four lines at once.
4. **Callback lag.** The call gets logged and returned three hours later. By then the customer booked someone else. This is the leak most owners underestimate because the call shows up as answered in the phone log.

Pull your carrier call log or your FSM call records and bucket last month's unanswered calls into those four rows. The distribution usually surprises people, and it is the difference between buying an answering service and fixing your dispatch process.

## What answering services cost and how they bill

**Answering services bill in incompatible units, which makes quotes hard to compare. Some charge per minute, some per call, some per booked appointment, and AI voice agents usually charge a flat monthly rate plus usage. Normalize every quote to cost per answered call before you compare anything.**

| Model | How you get billed | Fits | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Live operator, per minute | Minute bundles with overage rates | Emergency trades with short triage calls | Long calls and hold time burn the bundle fast |
| Live operator, per call | Flat rate each answered call | Predictable, low call volume | Wrong numbers and robocalls may still count |
| Per booked appointment | Only pay when a job lands on the calendar | Owners who want risk shifted to the vendor | Higher unit price, and quality of bookings varies |
| AI voice agent | Monthly platform fee plus per-minute usage | High volume, repetitive intake and scheduling | Handling of edge cases and true emergencies |
| In-house after-hours CSR | Wages plus payroll burden | Shops with enough volume to keep a person busy | Coverage gaps for sick days and vacation |

Ask every vendor the same three questions: what counts as a billable call, what happens on overage, and can they write into your scheduling system or only take a message. The in-house versus outsourced versus AI tradeoff is broken down in more depth in the [HVAC answering service comparison](/guides/hvac-answering-service/), and the same framework applies to plumbing, electrical, garage door, and septic.

## The break-even calculation, step by step

**Break-even is one division problem: monthly service cost divided by value per answered call equals the number of rescued calls you need. If your missed-call log shows more than that number in a typical month, the service pays. If it shows fewer, you are subsidizing coverage you do not need yet.**

Run it in this order:

1. **Calculate value per answered call** using the worksheet above.
2. **Count genuinely missed calls last month**, excluding spam, wrong numbers, and existing customers who reached you another way.
3. **Estimate the rescue rate.** Not every answered call converts at your normal rate. Assume the service performs worse than your own office until proven otherwise.
4. **Get the all-in monthly quote** including setup, overage, and any per-booking fee.
5. **Divide.** Monthly cost divided by value per answered call gives your break-even call count.
6. **Compare** break-even calls against step two.

Illustrative worked example using the table above: a $400 monthly quote divided by $140 value per answered call equals about 2.9 calls. If your log shows 18 genuinely missed calls a month, that is not a close decision. If it shows three, you are near the line and should fix your daytime routing first.

## When an answering service is the wrong fix

**An answering service captures calls. It does not create technician capacity, fix pricing, or repair a broken schedule. If your calendar is already full three weeks out and you turn work away, paying someone to answer more calls buys you a longer list of customers you will disappoint.**

Skip or delay the service when:

- Your close rate on calls you already answer is weak. That is a pricing, scripting, or estimating problem, and no third party fixes it for you.
- Most inbound calls are existing customers rescheduling. Self-serve booking and automated reminders handle that cheaper, which is the kind of leak a [dispatch-to-invoice process audit](/guides/how-to-automate-business-processes/) is built to find.
- You cannot actually dispatch after hours. Booking a 10pm emergency you have nobody to run is worse than not answering.
- Your missed-call count is in the low single digits and concentrated in one predictable window. Fix the routing in your [technician scheduling software](/guides/scheduling-software-for-service-technicians-2026/) first.

Honest limitation on the math itself: value per answered call is an average, and averages hide the tail. A single commercial account or a full system replacement can distort a 90 day sample. Run the numbers with and without your largest two jobs and see whether the decision changes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many calls does an answering service need to save to pay for itself?

Divide the all-in monthly cost by your gross profit per answered call. Using the illustrative figures above, a $400 monthly service breaks even at roughly three rescued calls. Your number will differ because it depends entirely on your job margins and close rate.

### Should the answering service book directly on my schedule?

Only if it can write into your actual system and respect your capacity rules, drive times, and skill requirements. Message-taking is safer than bad bookings. Start with triage and message-taking, then grant scheduling access once you trust the call quality.

### Is an AI answering service better than a live operator for contractors?

AI handles repetitive intake and after-hours scheduling well and costs less at volume. Live operators handle angry customers, unusual emergencies, and judgment calls better. Many shops run AI for overflow and a human for after-hours emergencies.

### What should I track to know if it is working?

Track calls answered, appointments booked, jobs actually completed from those bookings, and revenue attributed to them. Review monthly against the invoice. If booked-to-completed conversion is poor, the service is filling your calendar with junk.

### Do I need 24/7 coverage or just overflow?

Bucket last month's missed calls by time of day first. Emergency trades usually justify after-hours coverage. Scheduled-work trades like lawn care, painting, and fencing more often need daytime overflow only, which costs less.
