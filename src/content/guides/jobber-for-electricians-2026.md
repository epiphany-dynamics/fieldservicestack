<h2>What Electricians Actually Need in Field Service Software</h2>

<p><strong>Electrical contractors need software that handles variable-scope jobs, field invoicing, multi-technician dispatch, and materials tracking. A job that starts as a breaker swap and turns into a three-day rewire is normal in this trade. Generic scheduling apps built for any service business tend to fall apart fast when that happens.</strong></p>

<p>Running a two-truck electrical operation is not the same as running a cleaning crew with a fixed checklist. Your techs show up for a panel inspection and find aluminum wiring throughout the house. Now it's a five-figure job with a permit pull and two inspection hold points. Your software needs to handle that scope change, keep the customer informed, and still get a clean invoice out the same day the job closes.</p>

<p>Before committing to any platform, run through these questions:</p>

<ul>
<li>Can your techs invoice from their phone on-site, including adding materials they picked up at the supply house?</li>
<li>Does the scheduling view show all your guys on one screen without clicking into individual calendars?</li>
<li>Is there a flat-rate price book, or are you rebuilding every estimate from scratch?</li>
<li>Does it sync with QuickBooks automatically, or does someone have to export a CSV every Friday?</li>
<li>How long does onboarding take for a tech who's not comfortable with technology?</li>
</ul>

<p>If a platform can't answer those cleanly, keep looking. The good news is that a handful of options have figured out what electrical contractors actually need. Here's how the top five compare.</p>

<h2>The Top 5 Software Options for Electrical Contractors in 2026</h2>

<p><strong>The five strongest platforms for electrical contractors in 2026 are Jobber, Housecall Pro, ServiceTitan, FieldEdge, and Workiz. Jobber and Housecall Pro dominate for small shops. ServiceTitan is built for larger operations with the budget to match. FieldEdge handles inventory better than any of them. Workiz is the budget entry point for solo operators.</strong></p>

<h3>1. Jobber: Best All-Around for Small to Mid-Size Electrical Shops</h3>

<p>Jobber is the name that comes up most often when smaller electrical contractors start looking for software, and it earns that. The interface is clean, the mobile app actually works in the field, and a new tech can be productive on it within a day or two. That last part matters more than people give it credit for. If your guys hate using the software, you're back to text messages and sticky notes within a month.</p>

<p>For a shop running three to five electricians, the Connect plan at $129/month is usually the right landing spot. You get scheduling, dispatching, invoicing, client history with job photos, two-way QuickBooks Online sync, and automated follow-up messages to customers after a job closes. Online booking is included too, which matters if you're running any paid ads or getting organic traffic to your website.</p>

<p>The weak spots are real and worth knowing. Jobber's price books are basic. You can build a list of standard line items, but if you want a flat-rate catalog with hundreds of pre-built electrical tasks already in it, you're going to spend time building that yourself. Housecall Pro and FieldEdge come with more developed pricing tools. There's also no permit tracking natively, so you're managing that in a spreadsheet or somewhere separate. If your business does significant commercial work with permit pulls and inspection hold points, that gap will show up quickly.</p>

<p>Jobber offers three tiers: Core at $49/month for solo operators, Connect at $129/month for up to five users, and Grow at $249/month for up to fifteen. For a detailed breakdown of what you're actually paying for at each level, the <a href="/reviews/jobber-review-2026">Jobber Review 2026: The Best FSM Software for Small Teams?</a> covers that thoroughly.</p>

<p>Best for residential electrical service shops with 1-10 techs, and owners who want something they can learn without a dedicated IT person or a multi-month implementation process.</p>

<h3>2. Housecall Pro: Best for Flat-Rate Residential Electrical Shops</h3>

<p>If your operation runs the same jobs repeatedly (panel replacements, EV charger installs, outlet upgrades, service upgrades, lighting swaps), Housecall Pro's flat-rate price books change the math on every estimate your techs write. You build out your standard tasks with fixed prices, and your guys can put together a quote on the job site without doing mental math or calling the office to get approval on a number.</p>

<p>The customer-facing side is strong too. Automated review requests after job completion, two-way text with customers, and a clean customer portal all come standard on paid plans. If you're competing with larger, more established electrical contractors in your market and want to look more polished than your current setup, Housecall Pro helps close that gap.</p>

<p>Pricing sits at $59/month for one user and $149/month for up to five users, which is comparable to Jobber at the same crew size. The deciding factor between them usually comes down to your billing model. Hourly and time-and-materials shops tend to prefer Jobber's flexibility. Shops that have moved to flat-rate pricing lean toward Housecall Pro. The full head-to-head is in our <a href="/comparisons/jobber-vs-housecall-pro">Jobber vs Housecall Pro 2026: Which One Wins?</a> comparison if you want to dig into the specifics before making a call.</p>

<p>Best for residential electrical operations doing repeatable service work where flat-rate pricing is already (or should be) the standard billing method.</p>

<h3>3. ServiceTitan: Best for Larger Electrical Operations</h3>

<p>ServiceTitan is in a different category than the other platforms here, both in what it can do and what it costs. If you're running 10 or more techs, mixing commercial contracts with residential service, and want reporting that shows you exactly which job type makes you the most money per hour, ServiceTitan can produce that. It has the most capable dispatch board, the strongest profitability reporting, and the most integration options of any platform in this space.</p>

<p>The cost is the real obstacle. Pricing typically starts around $400/month and climbs based on users and the modules you add. Implementation is a multi-month process, not a two-week trial. You don't log in on Monday and start dispatching jobs on Tuesday.</p>

<p>For a shop at 12-15 techs currently running on a whiteboard and QuickBooks, ServiceTitan might be the right call. For a five-tech shop turning $700k a year, it's almost certainly overkill. The <a href="/comparisons/servicetitan-vs-jobber-2026">ServiceTitan vs Jobber 2026: Which One Fits Your Business?</a> comparison lays out exactly where that line falls.</p>

<p>Best for electrical contractors with 10 or more technicians, significant commercial work, and a realistic budget to support the investment.</p>

<h3>4. FieldEdge: Best for Electrical Shops with Real Inventory Needs</h3>

<p>FieldEdge doesn't get talked about as often as Jobber or Housecall Pro, but it's built specifically for HVAC and electrical contractors, and that focus shows in the features. The materials and inventory tracking is more developed here than anywhere else on this list. If you're stocking wire, breakers, panels, conduit, and specialty parts across multiple trucks or a shop location, FieldEdge handles that in ways Jobber and Housecall Pro simply don't.</p>

<p>Pricing is quote-based. Expect to land in the range of $100-150 per user per month depending on your configuration, which puts it closer to ServiceTitan territory for larger teams. The trade-off is a platform actually built for your trade instead of a horizontal tool that also happens to serve plumbers, cleaners, and landscapers. For the full breakdown, the <a href="/reviews/fieldedge-review-2026">FieldEdge Review 2026: Features, Pricing, Pros and Cons</a> is worth reading before you request a demo.</p>

<p>The learning curve is steeper than Jobber. Budget more onboarding time. Best for electrical contractors who are already feeling the pain of managing parts inventory outside their FSM software.</p>

<h3>5. Workiz: Best for Solo Electricians Watching Costs</h3>

<p>If you're a solo operator doing six to ten calls a day and your current system is a Google Calendar and hand-written invoices, Workiz gives you scheduling, invoicing, customer history, and payment collection at a price that's easier to justify when you're still building volume. The Business plan runs $150/month flat for up to five users, which is competitive with Jobber's Core tier for a small team.</p>

<p>The platform is less polished than Jobber and the feature set doesn't match Housecall Pro's pricing tools or Jobber's mobile experience. But for a solo electrician who needs to get organized without committing to $130/month before the business volume is there, it's a legitimate starting point. You can migrate to Jobber or Housecall Pro once the operation grows and the investment makes more sense.</p>

<p>Best for solo electricians and 1-2 person shops where keeping monthly software costs under $150 is a real constraint right now.</p>

<h2>How to Choose the Right Software for Your Electrical Business</h2>

<p><strong>Crew size and job mix drive the decision. Solo operators and small residential shops land on Jobber or Housecall Pro. Shops with real inventory complexity should look at FieldEdge before committing. Only run a ServiceTitan demo if your headcount and revenue can support the price tag without the investment being a stretch.</strong></p>

<p>Here's how it breaks down by where you are right now:</p>

<p>Solo electrician doing residential service calls: your range is $49-59/month. Jobber's Core plan handles the basics. If you already know you want flat-rate pricing built in, start with Housecall Pro at $59/month instead of building it from scratch later.</p>

<p>Two to five trucks doing residential and light commercial: you're looking at $129-149/month with Jobber Connect or Housecall Pro Essentials. The choice between them usually comes down to billing model. Time-and-materials shops tend to prefer Jobber's flexibility. Flat-rate shops lean Housecall Pro. Both are close enough that a two-week trial of each will tell you more than any comparison article.</p>

<p>Five to ten techs starting to feel the pain of your current setup (invoices going out three days late, techs unclear on what's on the schedule, no visibility into which jobs are actually profitable): you're in the zone where Jobber's Grow plan at $249/month or Housecall Pro's upper tiers make sense. If parts inventory is a constant headache, take a serious look at FieldEdge before you sign anything.</p>

<p>Ten or more techs, significant commercial work: run a ServiceTitan demo. The price is real, but so is the reporting gap between ServiceTitan and everyone else at this crew size. Flying blind on job costing when you're running 12 trucks is an expensive problem to ignore.</p>

<p>One thing worth checking regardless of which platform you're evaluating: make sure your techs can invoice on-site the same day a job closes, and that the invoice syncs to QuickBooks automatically. If either of those answers is "not exactly" or "there's a workaround," that friction will compound every single week.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Q: How much does Jobber cost compared to its main competitors for electrical contractors?</h3>
<p>Jobber's Connect plan is $129/month and includes scheduling, invoicing, client history, and two-way QuickBooks sync. Housecall Pro and FieldEdge typically range $99–$149/month depending on features, making them similarly priced; Jobber's automatic QuickBooks integration often justifies the cost for contractors already using that accounting platform.</p>
<h3>Q: Can electricians on Jobber invoice for unexpected scope changes before leaving the job site?</h3>
<p>Yes — Jobber's mobile app lets field technicians add materials and modify line items in real time before sending the invoice to the customer. This is critical for electrical work where a simple panel inspection can uncover aluminum wiring or code violations and turn into a multi-day, high-value project.</p>
<h3>Q: Does Jobber sync with QuickBooks automatically, or is manual data entry required?</h3>
<p>Jobber offers true two-way QuickBooks sync with the Connect plan, pushing invoices automatically and pulling account data back without manual export. This eliminates the Friday CSV export process that many contractors still handle with competing platforms.</p>
<h3>Q: How quickly can a non-tech electrician become productive with Jobber's mobile app?</h3>
<p>Most field electricians master core functions—daily schedule checks, on-site invoicing, customer notes—within a single shift with minimal training. The app is purpose-built for contractors rather than generic service businesses, so adoption tends to be faster than traditional scheduling software.</p>
