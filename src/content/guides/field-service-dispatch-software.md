**Field service dispatch software assigns jobs to technicians, routes them, and keeps every schedule current as the day changes. The right platform matches your crew size, trade, and job volume. Small shops need simple scheduling and invoicing; larger operations need routing, capacity planning, and reporting. Match the pricing shape to how you actually book work.**

Choosing dispatch software is a transactional decision, not a research project. You already feel the pain: a technician sitting idle across town while a rush callback stacks up, or a dispatcher rekeying the same job into three screens. This guide walks you through what dispatch software does on a real day, the features worth paying for, who each tier fits, and how to read pricing before you sign.

## What dispatch software actually does on a busy day

**Dispatch software is the control tower between the phone ringing and the invoice getting paid. It takes a booked job, assigns it to the right technician, sends them the address and history, tracks status in the field, and pushes the completed work into invoicing. Everything else layered on top is convenience around that core loop.**

Picture a Tuesday morning at a five-truck HVAC shop. Three maintenance visits are pre-booked, then a no-cool callback comes in at 9:15. Without software, your dispatcher calls each tech to ask who is closest, guesses at drive time, and scribbles the change on a whiteboard. With dispatch software, the callback drops onto a schedule board, you see which technician finishes nearest the new address, you drag the job onto their route, and their phone updates instantly with the customer's equipment history.

The same loop applies whether you run plumbing, appliance repair, pest control, or electrical. The trades differ; the dispatch-to-invoice motion does not. ## Features that matter when you dispatch every morning

**Prioritize a live schedule board, drag-and-drop reassignment, mobile job access, and automatic invoicing. Those four cover 80 percent of daily dispatch pain. Routing optimization, capacity limits, and reporting matter more as truck count grows. Treat customer texting, GPS tracking, and estimating as strong nice-to-haves, not deal-breakers, for a small crew.**

Not every feature earns its keep. Here is how to weigh the common ones against your day.

### Core features to insist on

- **Live schedule board:** you see every technician's day at a glance and reassign in seconds when a callback lands.
- **Mobile technician app:** techs pull up job notes, capture photos, collect signatures, and close out work from the driveway.
- **Estimate-to-invoice flow:** an approved estimate becomes an invoice without retyping line items, so cash does not leak between the truck and the office.
- **Customer history:** the last visit, the equipment installed, and the balance owed show up when the job opens.

### Features to weigh by crew size

- **Route optimization:** genuinely valuable at four or more trucks; marginal for a two-person shop that works one zip code.
- **Capacity planning:** stops your board from booking eight jobs into a six-slot day. Matters most for high-volume maintenance work.
- **Reporting and dashboards:** close rate, revenue per technician, and callback rate. Small shops rarely act on these; growing ones live in them.

## Who each type of dispatch software fits

**Match the tool to your stage. Solo operators and two-truck shops want a simple app that handles scheduling, estimates, and invoicing without a setup project. Growing five-to-fifteen-truck businesses need routing, dispatching by skill, and reporting. Enterprise operations with dozens of trucks need capacity planning, payroll integration, and deep customization. Buying above your stage wastes money and onboarding time.**

Use this table to place yourself before you demo anything.

| Business stage | Trucks | What you actually need | What to skip for now |
|---|---|---|---|
| Solo or side operation | 1 to 2 | Simple scheduling, mobile invoicing, card payments | Route optimization, advanced reporting |
| Growing shop | 3 to 8 | Drag-and-drop dispatch, customer texting, estimating | Enterprise customization, payroll add-ons |
| Established multi-crew | 8 to 20 | Skill-based dispatch, routing, revenue reporting | Full ERP integration |
| Enterprise | 20+ | Capacity planning, integrations, custom workflows | Entry-level all-in-one tools |

The mistake operators make is buying for the business they want in two years instead of the one dispatching today. A ten-truck feature set on a three-truck shop means paying for modules nobody opens and training techs on screens they do not need. ## How dispatch software pricing is actually shaped

**Dispatch software pricing comes in three shapes: flat per-user monthly fees, tiered plans that unlock features as you climb, and quote-only enterprise pricing. Per-user pricing scales with headcount, so seasonal crews get expensive fast. Tiered plans hide the features you want behind higher tiers. Always price the plan that includes what you actually need, not the sticker tier.**

Do not compare the advertised starting price. Compare the price of the plan that includes the features from your must-have list above. A platform advertising a low entry tier often gates estimating, dispatching to multiple techs, or QuickBooks sync behind the next tier up.

Three pricing traps to check before signing:

1. **Per-user creep:** a plan at an illustrative $40 per user reads cheap at two users and stings at ten, especially if you add seasonal help. Ask how inactive or seasonal users are billed.
2. **Feature gating:** confirm the specific feature you are buying for lives in the tier you are quoting, not the one above it.
3. **Payment processing spread:** many platforms bundle card processing and take a percentage. On high ticket jobs that spread can outweigh the subscription itself.

## A five-step framework to choose your dispatch software

**Work the decision in order: define your daily dispatch pain, list must-have versus nice-to-have features, place your business stage, shortlist two or three platforms, then run a real week in a trial. Skipping the trial is the most expensive shortcut, because a demo shows the happy path, not your Tuesday callback chaos.**

1. **Name the pain.** Write the one dispatch problem costing you money now: idle drive time, missed callbacks, or slow invoicing. Buy for that first.
2. **Split your feature list.** Mark each feature must-have or nice-to-have using the sections above. Do not let a slick demo promote a nice-to-have into a deal-maker.
3. **Place your stage.** Use the truck-count table so you shop the right tier.
4. **Shortlist two or three.** More than three stalls the decision. Pull from trade-specific roundups so the options already fit your work.
5. **Run a real week.** Load actual jobs, dispatch a live callback, and invoice one completed job in the trial before you commit.

## When dispatch software is not the fix

**Dispatch software will not save a business whose real problem is upstream. If jobs are not getting booked, calls are going to voicemail, or your pricing is guesswork, a schedule board just organizes a broken pipeline. Fix the intake and estimating gaps first, or the software becomes an expensive digital whiteboard that reflects the same chaos.**

Software organizes work you already have. If your phone coverage is the leak, no dispatch board recovers a job that never got booked, and the honest first move is your intake process, not a new subscription. Run the intake and estimating audit before you shop so the tool you buy solves the bottleneck you actually have.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between dispatch software and field service management software?

Dispatch software focuses on the assignment and scheduling loop: getting the right technician to the right job. Field service management software is the broader category that wraps dispatch together with CRM, estimating, invoicing, and reporting. Most modern platforms sold today are full field service management tools that include dispatch as one core module.

### Do I need dispatch software for a two-technician shop?

Yes, once you are juggling more than a handful of jobs a day. Even a two-truck shop benefits from a shared schedule, mobile invoicing, and one customer history everyone can see. Buy at the simple end of the market. A two-person crew rarely needs route optimization or advanced reporting, so paying for those wastes money.

### How much should dispatch software cost per month?

Pricing varies by tier and headcount, so treat any single number as illustrative. Entry plans are billed per user monthly, mid-market plans bundle more features per seat, and enterprise platforms quote after a demo. Always price the plan that includes your must-have features rather than the advertised starting tier, and factor in payment processing fees.

### Can dispatch software integrate with QuickBooks?

Most established platforms sync with QuickBooks Online, but the depth varies. Some push invoices and payments only, while others sync customers, line items, and payroll data. Confirm the specific sync direction and fields during your trial, because a shallow integration still leaves your bookkeeper doing double entry every week.

### What should I test during a free trial?

Load real jobs, not sample data. Dispatch a live callback by reassigning a technician mid-day, have a tech close out a job from the mobile app, and turn one completed job into a paid invoice. If those three actions feel clunky in a trial, they will feel worse under Tuesday morning pressure.
